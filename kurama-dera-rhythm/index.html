Kurama-dera Rhythm Analysis

A drawing-based simulation of the rhythms between Kurama Mountain and those who built a temple upon it.

Right at the edge of the lawn, facing the forest, was the entrance to my den. Here I gathered stones, organized snail races, and when a friend came by we went into the forest to look for new inventory. We found rusty pipes from a well system from the fifties, plastic bags, a device with cogwheels, a car tyre we turned into a swing, a strangely shaped root. Trip after trip the things were carried back and arranged within the den's own logic. Where the garden and the forest met and suggested a path, I marked it with stones. The terrain proposed a hierarchy: the highest spot, which received the most sun, became the living zone; a lower opening became a storage chamber.1

Through the den's collection of objects, the forest's temporality began to condense in one place, and the terrain stepped forward as legible spatialities and functions.

The den became what Christian Norberg-Schulz in Place describes as a foci: a gathering point where the character of a place is condensed and interpreted.2 Character is, he writes, the most immediate quality of a place — its atmosphere — and it is through this that one can approach genius loci. Drawing on the Roman understanding of genius as what a thing is or will be, he describes genius loci as the spirit of a place: that which determines its character and essence.3

What interests me about the den is not the finished form, where the spirit of the place was finally manifested, but rather the process through which I, in constructing the den, sensed a tension between my will and the will of the place — with inspiration from the ancient Romans: a tension between genius.

I am curious about how such a tension can be experienced — not as a fixed quantity, but as something that unfolds over time.

In The Textility of Making, Tim Ingold offers a conceptual apparatus for understanding the tension that arises between the form-giver and what is formed. One must in fact begin by dissolving the idea of a form-giver and something being formed. Ingold criticizes the hylomorphic model, where form-giving is understood as the realization of a predetermined idea in a passive material.4 Instead he points to form-giving as something that is not merely carried out, but that develops spontaneously and reactively in the meeting between forces.

In Ingold, the meeting between forces is rhythmic. Drawing on Henri Lefebvre, he describes rhythm as an interplay between repetition and variation — a repetition that is adjusted in response to the resistances it encounters. Sawing through wood is given as an example: a movement that repeats, but is constantly adjusted as the wood and the saw encounter resistance from each other.5 The idea of resistance as fundamental to form-giving can be nuanced through Fei Fei Zhou's conceptual pair of friction and care. In "What Makes a Drawing Spatial?" Zhou compares spatial analysis with sparring in Muay Thai as practices that both consist of an interplay between friction and care. Friction denotes the confrontation that creates intensity and drives the process forward, while care keeps that intensity from becoming destructive. Zhou illustrates this through images of martial arts practitioners where it is difficult to determine whether they are fighting against or taking care of each other.6 The contact thus appears simultaneously violent and tender, and it is precisely in this tension that learning becomes most effective.

Friction and care, in Zhou's spatial analysis, can be understood as qualities in the meeting between different actors and the place she examines. My investigation is directed toward how these qualities can emerge as rhythms in the meeting between the intervening and the background-creating actor's will. To this end, Kurama-dera is examined: a temple complex in the mountain forest north of Kyoto where construction and transformation across centuries have been continuous processes, and where the intervening and the background-creating mutually command great attention.

Since I cannot construct at Kurama, drawing is used as a simulation practice. In Ingold, drawing is understood as a trace of movement — a temporal and open process rather than a finished representation.7 In Zhou, friction and care are similarly experienceable in the act of drawing itself as resistance, adjustment, and attention. Drawing thus does not simulate the place as image, but as encounter. In this sense, drawing becomes not merely an analytical tool but a practice that corresponds to construction: a way of working into the rhythms of a place through alternating friction and care.

Historical and Spatial Context of Kurama-dera

Kurama-dera is a temple complex located in the Kurama mountains north of Kyoto, founded in 770 CE.8 The complex today belongs to its own independent order, Kurama-kokyō, but has historically drawn on layers of Buddhist traditions as well as older mountain religious practices.9

The complex is not a single temple but a collection of shrines, water features, stairways, and buildings that extend along a path of movement across the mountain ridge between the two villages of Kurama and Kibune.

The complex has two access points. From the Kurama side, one arrives through a gate at the mountain's foot, in its present form built in 1911,10 and from there ascends via a winding path system that follows the terrain. From the Kibune side, one arrives via a bridge over the Kibune river and through a western gate, from which the path leads up to the innermost sanctuary area.11

Analysis

Study Field 1 — Arrival at the Bridge

Figure 1. Drawing of the bridge at the arrival to Kurama-dera in three stages.

Text notes on the drawing process — Study Field 1

In the first layer of the drawing — watercourse, terrain, and trees — the wills of the place stepped clearly forward. The watercourse wanted terracing; there was an urge toward stepped levels, toward the water resting in basins on its way down. The trees wanted toward the sun and pulled the drawing's lines in particular directions. This produced a continuous, directional movement in the hand.

The human interventions appeared in the drawing with a different logic: repetition over direction. The fence posts, the windows of the multi-storey building, the square stones laid as steps down toward the water — all of this organized itself as series. Human and nature even wanted the same thing at times: a wall behind a rock revealed itself as wanting upward with it; the direction of the steps toward the water answered the water's own stair-logic. Yet there was a difference in how the two wills manifested themselves.

The friction in the drawing arose most clearly when oil pastel was introduced to draw the bridge. The line established itself without resistance — it went too fast. The forms could not be corrected, and what lay in front of the bridge could not be concealed. Care then had to step in as a subsequent adjustment: relations between foreground and background were re-established without the bridge disappearing beneath them.

Another form of care arose during the drawing of the reflection in the water. The bridge's pillars — normally drawn vertically — were here drawn horizontally; the wrist shifted without my deciding it. The movement extended itself, took time over each pillar, drew it at an entirely different pace.

Study Field 2 — The Gong at the Temple

Figure 2. Drawing of the gong at the main temple in four stages.

Text notes on the drawing process — Study Field 2

The tree in the foreground of the square formed a curved space: roots and crown mirror each other and together with the terrain form an arch. The hand followed this almost automatically — a continuous, organic movement.

The stone lantern broke this rhythm. Its form is built up in layered, horizontal displacements, and drawing it required repeated horizontal movements upward — one layer at a time. It felt almost unnatural: to move upward by laying horizontal strokes. The friction here lay not in nature versus architecture as opposites, but in two different ways of organizing movement — the curved continuous and the incrementally built.

The paving offered a parallel friction. The loose small stones on the mountainside were drawn as an almost fluid structure; the large square stones on the path established precise boundaries. The square stone surface around the lantern was particularly interesting: it is made of the same small stones as the mountainside, but organized in a square — same material, new order. It references both systems and distinguishes itself from both.

Care stepped forward when the branches in front of the temple roof had to be drawn. They could not be hidden; the roof had to remain visible through layers of pencil. It was not an elimination, but a negotiation: layers exist simultaneously, and the relation between them is restored rather than cleaned. A similar restraint showed itself in working with the red colour on the pavilion: the red is kept clear of both ground and roof by means of plinth and eaves. In the drawing it does not appear as dominance, but as a controlled marking within the place.

Finally: the gong and the wooden post. The post was erased and redrawn, repeatedly. The bell is experienced as both stable and vibrating. The friction lay in the attempt to draw something temporal and resonant — the sound moving out into the square's curved terrain — in a static medium.

Qualification of Rhythm

Across the two study fields, three rhythmic structures emerge.

Direction versus repetition. The landscape's forces manifest in the drawing as continuous, oriented lines — the direction of water, the trees' reach toward light. The human interventions manifest as series: fence posts, storeys, paving stones. Rhythm arises in the tension between these two logics, where they either reinforce or work against each other. In Lefebvre, this can be described as an encounter and a polyrhythmia12 between a cyclical rhythm13 — covering nature's great cosmic and organic movements — and a linear rhythm covering the human, social, and stringent systems of repetition.14 Both are rhythmic, but of different types. When these two logics work against each other in the drawing, the tension tips into arrhythmia.15

Speed and resistance in the medium. The shift between pencil and oil pastel created two different temporalities in the drawing process: the pencil's slow, resistant build-up and the oil pastel's rapid establishment of connections. These material differences are not neutral — they change what can be experienced. The friction of the oil pastel arose paradoxically precisely in its absence: too little resistance gave too little time and thus too little opportunity for care. This is a self-contradiction, and in resisting it — by later adding details where the oil pastel had erased them — this rhythm can be qualified as polyrhythmia, which is precisely what this concerns.16

Stability and vibration. The gong and the lantern appear as stable and heavy objects, yet are activated by sound, shadows, and movement. The attempt to draw the post again and again created a rhythm in the very repetition of the attempt. The drawing became the registration of a state between stillness and resonance — reflecting Lefebvre's thesis that even the inert and immobile field of objects in reality conceals inner vibrating energies and temporalities.17

Conclusion

Cultural Situating

Teiji Itoh describes Japanese architecture as a practice that sets limits to the infinite with the help of the landscape.18 Kurama-dera's constructions do not isolate themselves from the mountain but order it through borrowed scenery. The square stone surface around the lantern stages a paradox: it marks a precise demarcation of the infinite terrain, yet is made of the mountainside's own raw small stones.

Itoh points out that the vertical plane in the Japanese architectural tradition ought to be understood rhythmically.19 This was experienced in the drawing of the bridge: the vertical plane breaks and shifts when the wrist unconsciously changes tempo and draws the bridge's vertical pillars with long, horizontal movements in the water's reflection. The bridge moves in as a horizontal intervention and a transition, before the vertical mountain space again takes over.

Discussion

The question is whether the rhythmicities I qualify belong to the place or merely to the drawing. Here the concept of simulation is central: a simulation is not a repetition of the real, but an imitation with a controlled focus directed toward learning and analysis. This investigation functioned as an experimental field for my own will in the encounter with the paper's materiality and photographs from 2026.

The most fundamental critique of my analysis concerns the quality of the assignment's historical focus. The historical focus could be nuanced by replacing the contemporary background with a reconstruction of the original conditions. This would require research into geology, materials, cultural understandings of reality, and the practical challenges of construction itself — such as processing and transport. At the same time, the assignment contains a theoretical tension with Ingold, where the concept of actor is dissolved in favour of energy and relations between materials. To reconcile this with a classical actor model is not unproblematic, and the tension remains unresolved. Nevertheless, the simulation proves a concept: the analysis produces rhythmic qualities that are experienced through the drawing and relate to Kurama-dera.

Perspectives: Rhythm, AI, and the Future of Design

The assignment opens up an analytical method that investigates rhythmicity in the meeting between construction and place — not as a means toward a finished architectural product, but as an aesthetic object in itself. This is atypical for architecture, which traditionally regards process and method as merely the path toward the building. Artificial intelligence and generative design threaten precisely this traditional role. As AI improves at designing, the question arises of whether the architect's future function is to form the building at all — or whether the role must be fundamentally rethought.

It is in this light that game design becomes relevant. In building games, the player constructs within a place's own logics and resistances — a conversation between human and environment that recalls the rhythmicity at work in this assignment. No existing game consciously works with the rhythmicity of this conversation. But game design struggles with a classic conflict that Federico Campagna describes: narrative and game mechanics — ludology — pull in opposite directions, and the narrative most often wins at the expense of free play. Campagna describes play as an activity that requires mystique as its motivation — an inexplicable dimension that draws one forward, and which is destroyed if subjected to too rigid control.

Here, rhythmicity opens onto something deeper than simply better game design. AI could function as a rhythmic autotune — not to optimize the experience, but to allow the construction's meeting with the place to resonate with ontological patterns. A curated yet adaptive reflection on the relationship between human and cosmos — not instructed, but facilitated, so that whoever builds finds their own way into a larger conversation.
