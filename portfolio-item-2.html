The Stair

A speculative architectural response to climate catastrophe.

This dystopian fiction imagines my home city of Aarhus 70 meters underwater - an impossible scenario inspired by contemporary climate discourse. In this imagined future, The Stair becomes a monumental piece of survival infrastructure, allowing people to live above the flood line. The project explores extreme architectural responses through analogue design methods, later developed into digital visualizations.

Initial Sketches

The project started with loose sketches to explore the basic concept. Working with pencil on paper allows for quick iteration and helps develop the initial form and spatial relationships without getting caught up in technical details too early.

Plan Development

The sketches evolved into more structured plan drawings, still done by hand. This stage focused on working out the spatial organization and how people might actually use the structure. The plan shows the stair as a series of platforms and landings that could function as living spaces above the flood line.

Working Analogue

All the drawings shown here are hand-drawn. I prefer working this way during the early design phases because it keeps the focus on the big ideas rather than getting distracted by technical details. The drawings can be rough and exploratory, which suits this type of speculative project.

Digital Visualization

The next phase involves translating these hand-drawn concepts into digital renders. These will explore materiality, lighting, and atmosphere to better communicate how the spaces might feel to inhabit. The renders will show different perspectives of the stair structure and how communities might form within its vertical landscape.

Speculative Fiction as Design Tool

This project uses an impossible dystopian scenario as a framework for exploring extreme architectural responses. While 70 meters of sea level rise could never actually happen, the fiction allows for investigation of how architecture might adapt to catastrophic conditions and what forms survival infrastructure could take.
