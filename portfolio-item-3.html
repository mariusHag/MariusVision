My Second Semester Project

During the spring semester at the Royal Academy, our studies included reading about 'The Perfection of Myth'.

My program is a secular naming ritual — a place where one can meditate on naming as a phenomenon. Located as an extension to the old transformer station at Frederiksberg Hospital, it explores the deep paradox of identity.

To give a child a name is a necessary reduction: the child becomes a word, which allows them to enter a community, to be seen and loved. But it is also a limitation. There is a silent loss that happens every time humans try to hold onto the world. We name stars, diseases, emotions, children, love, and grief—as if the words can hold them still long enough so we don’t lose them. My architecture attempts to support a meditation on this very paradox.

The ritual starts upon arrival at the extension I added to the old transformer station. I removed the original door and repurposed it as a sliding door for the new building. You arrive at a reception and meet a guide. While waiting for the other guests, you look out towards two large trees—a Turkish hazel and an ash tree. In the reception, a ladder leads up to a roof terrace exclusively for the employees, where they gather leaves from these very trees to brew a tea used later in the ritual.

Once all 50 guests have arrived, the ritual begins. The employee leads the way and explains: "In the columns you will see how the architecture abstracts the trees — an infinitely complex living reality reduced to a simple diagram. This is what we are doing to this child today. As you walk through, prepare yourselves to understand this reduction."

Wandering between the columns, you meet a staircase leading down to an underground, organically shaped room made of black bricks. The room is filled with thick steam from a large pot of brewing tea, making it difficult to decipher the boundaries of the room and the shapes of the other guests. To design this complex geometry, I created a physical clay model, 3D scanned it, and generated the intricate brickwork using Grasshopper scripts.

The employee hands out the tea brewed from the roof terrace leaves and says: "Before the abstraction, there is the void. Before the word, you were a boundless form in the dark. Drink the tea. Meditate on the time before you had boundaries." After drinking the tea, you walk through a narrow, tight, and cold tunnel.

You then step out into a circular room, still underground, but with the ceiling entirely open to the sky. There are niches filled with folded paper lanterns, brushes, and ink. The employee continues: "Under the open sky, form returns. We cannot live in the infinite and formless. Write your name on a lantern, draw your boundaries into existence."

Everyone paints their name. The parents of the nameless child write "Aske" on a larger, red lantern. The employee lights an oil lamp, handing it to the parents who light the red lantern, and pass the flame along until the room is glowing with fifty paper lamps.

Finally, the procession ascends to a path that divides the garden in two: on one side, a perfectly manicured garden; on the other, a flooded landscape. The architecture forces you down close to the water's surface. The employee speaks at the water's edge: "The water does not abstract. It mirrors every twisted branch, every broken leaf. We place the name on the water as a promise to the child: we have given you a word to bind you to the world — but the world will still mirror you in your entirety."

The paper lanterns are set on the water, drifting out. Fifty names forming a constellation on the dark water, floating over the perfect reflections of tree crowns, which might just as well be roots.
