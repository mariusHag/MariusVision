Theory & History: Where is Alhambra — Physically and Metaphysically?

What does it truly mean for a building to "stand" somewhere? An exploration through landscape aesthetics and the ontology of architecture as event.

In 1492, Christopher Columbus stood in Alhambra in southern Spain and asked the Spanish queen for permission to sail west. The palace had just been reconquered, and the decision was made to preserve it — an Islamic paradise on earth that became the starting point for a mission that ultimately placed America on the world map. And later, the moon.

But where does such a mission begin — a mission that reconstructs reality and paradigms? Perhaps in the constructed itself: in Alhambra, which still stands today. What does it actually mean for a building to "stand" somewhere? Is it merely a question of geographical placement, or does the concept hold deeper layers of meaning?

This assignment examines Alhambra through two theoretical perspectives: Sven-Ingvar Andersson's landscape-aesthetic analysis of architectural placement,1 and Dinesen's ontological question about architecture's position.2 Where Andersson focuses on the visible relationship between building and landscape, Dinesen seeks to define architecture's essence in terms of construction and event.

The assignment's research question: Where is Alhambra — physically and metaphysically? This opens an inquiry on two levels. The physical layer concerns Alhambra's concrete placement in the landscape and the factors that have shaped it. The metaphysical layer concerns architecture's ontological position — where it "stands" in relation to questions of essence, construction, and event.

Methodologically, the assignment takes a phenomenological approach, examining architecture through concrete personal experiences. Alhambra is described as it appears from specific positions: Position A — the terrace of a teahouse by the river Darro; and Position B — the ascending movement of the stairway up toward the palace. Position C indicates the Alhambra complex itself.

Sitting by the River

From the teahouse terrace by the river Darro, immediately below Alhambra, the landscape unfolds in a vertical sequence that reveals itself slowly. Seated at a table, gaze directed toward Alhambra, the experience begins in the nearest layer.3 The river runs unseen beneath the terrace but is heard as a low, constant murmur between townhouses in Spanish Baroque style. The eye is drawn upward by a Moorish city wall that further down attempts to cross the river in an arch that breaks. The wall finally pulls the gaze upward. The first traces of the palace emerge between stone pines and cypresses, where walls and trees alternately conceal each other. Layer upon layer, the complex unfolds in a sequence reminiscent of Baroque landscape paintings4 — where buildings were freely placed in layers in the landscape to create a harmonious composition.

Andersson writes that a building is beautifully situated when we understand the context it finds itself in.5 It is evident that Alhambra sits high for defensive reasons, but it is only in the experienced sequence of layers — water, townhouses, wall, vegetation, palace — that the placement truly becomes apparent. Each line in the landscape reinforces the next: the city wall points upward, the tree trunks continue the movement, the masonry verticals lift the eye even higher. And behind Alhambra, the snow-capped peaks of the Sierra Nevada glitter as the final horizontal layer, completing the composition.

If the body turns so that its back is to Alhambra and the gaze is directed toward Granada itself, another relationship emerges. On the opposite hillside, the city descends in terracotta roofs and narrow streets, falling in a natural amphitheater. Alhambra sits high to look out over the city — but also to be seen from it. The Sultanate positioned itself where it could be seen from all of Granada. The position communicates power through visibility. Every citizen moving through the city's streets had the palace in their peripheral vision.

Behind Alhambra, the Sierra Nevada rises — snow-capped peaks marking a boundary. The Sultanate elevated itself above the people, yet remained below the mountains. In an Islamic worldview, one does not position oneself above God's creation. The mountains stand as a manifestation of divine creation, unchanging and beyond human power. The Sultanate took the position from which it could rule over people without raising itself above the God-made. Yet it placed itself in direct relation to the mountains — with the Sierra Nevada's peaks as backdrop, connecting itself to the creation in a way the people could not. A hierarchy written into the terrain itself.

Andersson's point — that power, placement, and production must not be separated — becomes concrete here.6 Alhambra sits high to defend, to oversee, and to be visible. The position was also chosen because the surrounding land could sustain production — the pomegranates that gave the city of Granada its name grew on the slopes. Water from the Sierra Nevada could be channeled down through the complex. According to Andersson, what is decisive for a building to be beautifully situated is that these functions — defense, production, representation — are inscribed in the same topographic position, not separated as distinct considerations. Seen from the teahouse, Alhambra unfolds precisely according to this logic.

The Stairway

The stairway up to Alhambra begins at a small square, on the other side of the river between the people's city and the Sultanate's city. To step onto the first tread is to enter a construction that is at once fixed and negotiating.

The first steps are low; the foot is placed naturally, the body moves upward without resistance, almost as on flat terrain. But already after ten to fifteen steps, the terrain begins to rise more steeply, and the treads change character. Now a greater lift of the body is required for each step, and the foot is placed differently — more on the forefoot, weight shifted further forward. This shift is not gradual, but marked. It is felt in the legs, in the breathing, in the body's attentiveness.

This variation in step height and depth is a direct response to the landscape's slope. The construction negotiates with the topography. Dinesen insists that architecture stands in the constructed7 — in the deliberately laid plan.8 And the steps are undeniably constructed. They are laid with intention, cut from stone, placed by decision. But they are not laid independently of the terrain they meet. The stairway is both intention and response. Both plan and negotiation.

Along the left handrail — the inner one — a thin layer of water runs constantly downward: a steady, low trickling that accompanies the ascent as an auditory line. When the body stops to rest, the water continues. The stairway is never entirely still. The water and the body's opposing movement is poetic yet simultaneously practical. In Granada's dry climate it is infrastructure. Water is led down from Alhambra's heights, through the stairway, onward to the city. The stairway becomes an aqueduct. But the effect is also sensory. The sound of trickling water marks the continuity of movement. Even when the foot stands still, the movement continues in the water. Time and place become interwoven. The stairway is both place and event.

Dinesen titles his text "Architecture's Place as Event."9 The stairway up toward Alhambra can nuance what it means to stand in event.

The Metaphysical Layer

From the plateau at Alhambra's entrance — after the whole stairway has been climbed — a new experience opens. Here the body does not turn toward the entrance, but back toward Granada. From this height, the city spreads beneath the observer like a map, revealing all the layers that before could only be sensed. The terracotta rooftops, the church spires, the narrow streets winding between buildings. But also strata of time — Moorish city walls beside Christian monasteries beside modern apartment blocks. The city is not a single historical moment, but many layers deposited one upon another.

This experience of temporal layers is not necessarily accessible from the teahouse terrace below. From there, Alhambra is seen as composition, as totality, as a fragment in the landscape. But from here — from the plateau — Granada is seen as what was surveyed from power. Two different positions, two different experiences of the same architectural situation. Alhambra is not located in one place, but in many places depending on where the body stands.

This points to something central in the understanding of architecture's essence. Dinesen places emphasis, in Architecture's Place as Event, on architecture standing in the constructed.10

Conclusion

In this assignment I have moved through Granada — from the terrace by the Darro, up the stairway, to the plateau at Alhambra. Through this movement I have gained a deeper understanding of why the palace is beautifully situated. It inscribes itself in a vertical system of layers — water, city, vegetation, palace, mountains — that creates a compositional whole that explains itself.

By ascending the stairway, I have step by step come to understand how construction negotiates with reality. The steps' shifting heights are responses to terrain. The water's course is a response to climate. The construction arises in the encounter with the forces it must negotiate with.

Through Dinesen's concept of "architecture's place as event" I have gained an understanding that Alhambra is located differently depending on where the body stands — as a view from the terrace, as resistance on the stairway, as a gaze from the plateau. The essence lies in the meeting between construction and body.

For me, therefore, architecture does not stand in one fixed place — but neither does it dissolve into symbolism or politics. It stands in that meeting where construction becomes event; which for me perhaps means something different than it does for Dinesen. The question of where Alhambra lies is thus a question of architecture's ontological position — experienced through the body's movement in space, a question that has made me more aware of what it means to lie beautifully, and of the consequences of experiencing what lies beautifully.
