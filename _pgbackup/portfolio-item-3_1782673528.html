My Second Semester Project

During the spring semester at the Royal Academy, our assignment was to design a ritual space.

My program is a secular naming ritual — a place where one can meditate on naming as a phenomenon. Located as an extension to the old transformer station at Frederiksberg Hospital, this project is fundamentally about transformation.

Our semester included reading The Perfection of Myth, which argues that a myth is simply a narrative through which we understand reality. These narratives have value as tools, but we should not mistake them for reality itself. Today, our rational understanding of the world is just another narrative that people have come to believe is absolute truth. The issue with this "rationality myth" is that it is strictly limited by language; everything must be reduced to words.

I resonate deeply with this critique because some of the most beautiful things in life simply cannot be explained with words. What a sad consequence, then, if even a child must be entirely reduced to a word in the act of naming. My architecture attempts to support a meditation on this very paradox, offering a space to reflect on the silent loss that occurs when we try to capture the boundless world in words.

The ritual starts upon arrival at the new extension. I removed the original door from the transformer station and repurposed it as a sliding door for the new building. You arrive at a reception and meet a guide. While waiting for the other guests, you look out towards a Turkish hazel and an ash tree. In the reception, a ladder leads up to a roof terrace exclusively for the employees, where they gather leaves from these trees to brew a tea used later in the ritual.

In all my technical drawings, the elements highlighted in red indicate where I have transformed the existing structure and added my design.

Once the guests are gathered, the ritual begins. The guide leads the way into a squared room, explaining: "In the columns you will see how the architecture abstracts the trees — an infinitely complex living reality reduced to a simple diagram. This is what we are doing to the child today. As you walk through, prepare yourselves to understand this reduction."

Wandering between the columns, you meet a staircase leading down to an underground, organically shaped room made of black bricks. To design this complex geometry, I created a physical clay model, 3D scanned it, and then generated the intricate brickwork parameters using various Grasshopper scripts.

Down in the underground room, the air is filled with thick steam from a large pot of brewing tea, making it difficult to decipher the boundaries of the space and the shapes of the other guests. The guide hands out the tea brewed from the roof terrace leaves: "Before the abstraction, there is the void. Before the word, you were a boundless form in the dark. Drink the tea. Meditate on the time before you had boundaries."

After drinking the tea, you walk through a narrow, tight, and cold tunnel. You then step out into a circular room, still underground, but with the ceiling entirely open to the sky. There are niches filled with folded paper lanterns, brushes, and ink.

The guide continues: "Under the open sky, form returns. We cannot live in the infinite and formless. Write a name on a lantern, draw the boundaries into existence."

The participants paint on the lanterns. A flame is passed along until the circular space is glowing with fifty paper lamps.

Finally, the procession ascends to a path that divides the garden in two: on one side, a perfectly manicured garden; on the other, a flooded landscape. The architecture forces you down close to the water's surface. The guide speaks at the water's edge:

"The water does not abstract. It mirrors every twisted branch, every broken leaf. We place the name on the water as a promise to the child: we have given you a word to bind you to the world — but the world will still mirror you in your entirety."

The paper lanterns are set on the water, drifting out into a constellation on the dark surface, floating over the perfect reflections of tree crowns that might just as well be roots.
